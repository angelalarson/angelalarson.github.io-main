Project Goal:

This project analyzes greenhouse gas emissions across U.S. states and territories using historical emissions inventories and wildfire emissions data. First, I aim to explore long-term trends in emissions across states between 1990 and 2022. Using time-series analysis, I will compare changes in total emissions over time and compare trends across emission sectors such as transportation, energy, and agriculture. I will also identify which sectors drive emissions in each state then use this to predict future state emissions. These analyses will provide a baseline understanding of the major drivers of greenhouse gas emissions across the United States.

Then, I want to specifically observe California state emissions and wildfire emissions. Despite wildfire emissions being included in each state greenhouse gas inventory, the use of a separate wildfire dataset provides event-level information such as acres burned and emissions per fire. I will compare wildfire emissions against other major emission sectors in California to better understand their relative contribution to total emissions. I intend to use a regression model to predict total California emissions using historical anthropogenic sector emissions and wildfire activity indicators. By comparing model predictions using anthropogenic emissions alone versus models that incorporate wildfire emissions and fire activity metrics, I aim to demonstrate how wildfire activity is increasingly contributing to California greenhouse gas emissions variability.

Description of Datasets:

The first dataset I will be using includes individual state and U.S. territory greenhouse gas emissions, grouped by greenhouse gas (Tg) and economic sector. These data range from years 1990-2022. Each row represents one greenhouse gas emission per year includes economic sector and subsectors, UNFCCC-IPCC sector and subsectors, carbon pool, fuel type, greenhouse gas type, and emission per year. This extensive dataset uses governmental language and is meant to inform emissions policies.

State greenhouse gas emissions data: U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) State Greenhouse Gas Inventory Dataset can be downloaded through the zip file under "Download state-level GHG data" section. https://www.epa.gov/ghgemissions/methodology-report-inventory-us-greenhouse-gas-emissions-and-sinks-state-1990-2022

The second dataset I will be using is California wildfire emissions data between the years of 2015-2024, compilied by the California Air Resources Board (CARB). Each row represents one wildfire event, represented by a unique wildfire ID and presents data on the number of acres burned, emissions, and fuel consumption data in addition to emissions per individual greenhouse gas (tons). These data are meant to support public knowledge of wildfire emissions and air quality risk.

California wildfire emissions data: California Air Resources Board (CARB) Wildfire Emissions Inventory Dataset can be downloaded by clicking on "Summary Table" under "Wildfire Emissions Data" section. https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/wildfire-emissions

One question I aim to answer is how wildfire emission trends and industry emission trends differ. To do so, I will observe emissions trends in 2020/21, where production emissions will likely be decreased due to the COVID-19 pandemic but wildfire emissions will likely be increased due to particularly tragic drought years in California through time series analysis.

While the all-state greenhouse gas emissions dataset includes a large breadth of data, the wildfire dataset is limited by its lack of national relevancy as who produced this dataset is the California-specific government sector California Air Resources Board. There is no standardized wildfire dataset to be found for each individual state, nor does this dataset exceed ten years worth of data. This has limited the depth of this data analysis and reproducibility per state.

        'geo_ref',
